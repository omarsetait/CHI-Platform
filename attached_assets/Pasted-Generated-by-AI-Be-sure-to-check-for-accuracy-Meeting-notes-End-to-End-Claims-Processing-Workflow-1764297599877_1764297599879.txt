End-to-End Claims Processing Workflow: 
Hanouf provided a comprehensive walkthrough of the claims journey, detailing each stage from pre-authorization submission by providers through validation, adjudication, settlement, and final payment, with active participation and clarifying questions from Zubin Pithawala and others.
	Pre-Authorization and Submission: Hanouf explained that providers submit pre-authorization requests, to which payers must respond within 60 minutes. Providers then have a set period (30 days for private, 45 for MLN, 60 for international) to submit claims after the service date, using the national platform Nafis, which covers over 94% of providers.
	Claims Validation and GSS Process: Claims are validated for technical and medical standards, with the payer responsible for ensuring no duplicates or invalid transactions. The General Summary Statement (GSS) process aligns provider billing with internal records, and only after GSS alignment are claims batched for further processing.
	Batch Creation and Medical Adjudication: Once claims are validated and batched, further checks are performed, including VAT invoice verification and early payment processing for eligible providers. Medical adjudication is performed by doctors, supported by system engines for automation, with manual review for complex cases.
	Settlement and Final Payment: After adjudication, senior regional managers conduct quality checks on batches before converting them to credit notes for finance. Final payments are made after reconciliation with providers, with the process varying based on contractual agreements.
	Automation and Manual Processing: Automation is applied to dental, optical, and some outpatient claims, with over 45% of claims processed automatically. Inpatient claims remain fully manual due to complexity, and the team is working towards increasing automation in the future.
Quality Assurance and Process Improvement: 
Hanouf and the team discussed quality control measures, sampling methodologies, and ongoing efforts to standardize decisions and improve the accuracy of claim approvals and rejections, with input from Zubin Pithawala and others on sampling, remedial actions, and areas for enhancement.
	Sampling and Quality Review: Quality checks are performed using a sampling approach, focusing on top rejection reasons and ensuring samples are representative by provider size and claim type. The aggregate sampling rate is typically 5-7%, with higher rates for smaller providers.
	Quality Pillars and Scoring: Three pillars are used for quality assessment: processor quality, provider statement quality, and system engine quality. Quality scores vary widely by provider and rejection reason, making aggregate scoring challenging.
	Standardization and Remedial Actions: A key area for improvement is standardizing adjudication decisions, particularly regarding cost discrepancies between pre-authorization and claims. The team is working to codify rules and cascade them across teams to reduce subjectivity.
	Root Causes and Technology Leverage: Manpower limitations and the inability to automate all validation steps are cited as root causes for missed rejections. The team is leveraging technology to filter claims and focus manual review on complex cases, with ongoing analysis to identify further automation opportunities.
Automation Versus Manual Claims Handling: 
Hanouf detailed the current state of automation in claims processing, highlighting which claim types are automated, the limitations for inpatient claims, and the ongoing efforts to expand automation, with clarifying questions from the team.
	Current Automation Coverage: Automation is fully implemented for dental, optical, and pharmaceutical claims, and covers about 45-50% of outpatient claims, with rules applied at the service level rather than the claim level.
	Manual Processing Scope: Inpatient claims and certain outpatient services require manual adjudication due to complexity and the need for specialized medical review, handled by a team of around 30 medical staff.
	Mass Processing Tool: A mass processing tool within Medco allows doctors to filter and batch process claims with obvious approval or rejection criteria, supporting efficiency but still requiring manual oversight.
	Future Automation Goals: The team aims to increase automation, particularly for outpatient claims, by developing more advanced rules and leveraging supporting documents and images, though full automation for inpatient claims remains a long-term objective.

	Non-Nafis Submissions: A small percentage of providers still submit claims outside Nafis, often through physical or intermediary platforms, but these are being phased out as integration progresses.
GSS Process and Pre-GSS Validation: 
Hanouf explained the General Summary Statement (GSS) process, emphasizing the importance of pre-GSS validation to ensure clean billing and reduce reconciliation efforts, with questions from the team about process duration and impact.
	Pre-GSS Validation Steps: Pre-GSS validation includes checks for duplicate transactions, member eligibility, policy validity, and prior approvals, aiming to ensure only valid claims proceed to GSS.
	GSS Workflow and Timelines: The GSS process typically takes 5-7 days, involving negotiation with providers to rectify errors before finalizing the statement, with delays often due to provider responsiveness.
