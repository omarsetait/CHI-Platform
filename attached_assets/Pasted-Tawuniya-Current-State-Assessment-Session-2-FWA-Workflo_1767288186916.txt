Meeting notes: 

Overview of Medical Audit and FWA Processes: Omar, Dr.Amr, and Arvind provided a comprehensive walkthrough of the medical audit team's structure, their dual approach to fraud, waste, and abuse (FWA) detection—manual provider auditing and automated rule-based systems—and the collaboration with internal stakeholders such as provider relations and clinical governance. 

Team Structure and Distribution: Omar explained that the medical audit team consists of approximately 15 members, primarily physicians, distributed across three regions, with responsibilities aligned to specific providers and targets. 

Manual Provider Auditing Process: Dr.Amr described the manual auditing process, which involves extracting claims data for assigned providers, analyzing trends and anomalies using tools like Oracle BI, SAS Enterprise Guide, Excel, and Power BI, and preparing findings for presentation to providers after internal alignment with governance and provider relations teams. 

Automated Rule-Based Auditing: Dr.Amr and Arvind detailed the use of SAS Visual Investigator for building and validating backend rules that automatically flag or reject claims based on predefined criteria, with around 50 rules currently live and a validation project underway to optimize accuracy. 

Stakeholder Collaboration: The team aligns findings and rule proposals with internal stakeholders, including the governance team for validation of medical guidelines and provider relations for communication and follow-up with providers. 

Technical Workflow of Claims and Pre-Authorization Processing: Omar, Dr.Amr, and Arvind clarified the technical workflow for claims and pre-authorization processing, distinguishing between the SAS-based automated rule engine for claims and the in-house Medgo rules for pre-authorization, and explained the data flow, decision points, and override capabilities. 

Claims Processing Flow: Claims are first validated in the Medgo system with pre-GSS and post-GSS checks; only those passing these checks are sent to SAS, which applies rule-based logic to flag or reject claims, with decisions sent back to Medgo and not overridable by claim processors. 

Pre-Authorization Workflow: Pre-authorization requests are processed in real time, with SAS providing advisory recommendations to pre-auth users, who can accept or override these suggestions, while the Medgo system also applies simpler, in-house rules for basic eligibility checks. 

Rule Creation and Validation: Rules for SAS are developed by the audit team, scripted by technical staff, tested on live and test data, and validated by the governance team before deployment; Medgo rules are managed separately by the clinical governance team. 

Data Handling and Batch Processing: SAS processes claims in daily batches, running its rule engine after Medgo validations, and immediately communicates rejection or flagging decisions to Medgo, ensuring real-time or near-real-time intervention in the claims workflow. 

Inter-Team Communication and Feedback Mechanisms: Omar and Dr.Amr discussed the bidirectional communication between the audit, pre-authorization, and provider relations teams, including the sharing of insights, manual notifications, and the use of system flags to coordinate on suspicious claims and provider behaviors. 

Manual and Automated Notifications: The audit team shares findings and alerts with the pre-authorization team via email and system notifications, while pre-auth users can flag suspicious cases in Medgo, which are routed to the audit team for further investigation. 

Use of System Flags: Medgo includes a fraud icon for pre-auth users to report suspected FWA cases, which triggers email notifications to the audit team, facilitating prompt review and potential rule updates. 

Feedback on Rule Effectiveness: The teams periodically review the effectiveness of SAS rules, discussing cases where rules may not have captured all relevant claims and considering adjustments or new rule proposals based on manual findings. 

Pain Points and Opportunities for Process Improvement: Omar, Dr.Amr, and other participants identified key pain points in the current audit process, including manual data extraction, time-consuming investigations, and limited automation, and discussed the potential for advanced analytics and AI to enhance efficiency and insight generation. 

Manual Data Extraction Challenges: Data extraction from Medgo is performed manually and on an ad hoc or periodic basis, depending on provider size and audit plans, with no fully standardized schedule, making it a significant bottleneck. 

Investigation and Analysis Workload: A large portion of the team's time is spent on data analysis, claim investigation, and preparing presentations for providers, with creativity and medical expertise driving much of the pattern recognition. 

Desire for Proactive and Automated Insights: Participants expressed the need for more proactive, AI-driven insights that could automatically identify patterns, anomalies, and trends, reducing reliance on manual analysis and enabling more timely interventions. 

Potential for OCR and Advanced Validation: There is interest in leveraging technologies like OCR to automate the validation of attachments and supporting documents, further streamlining the evidence-gathering process for provider presentations. 

KPIs, Prevention Strategies, and Recovery Actions: Omar and Dr.Amr outlined the team's current focus on retrospective savings as a KPI, ongoing efforts to shift toward preventive measures such as provider rehabilitation, and the processes for recovery and escalation in cases of confirmed fraud or abuse. 

Current and Evolving KPIs: The primary KPI is retrospective savings from FWA detection, but the team is working to increase emphasis on prevention, including provider behavior modification and monitoring. 

Provider Rehabilitation and Preventive Actions: Preventive strategies include provider rehabilitation programs, blocking of certain services or physicians, and ongoing education to reduce future incidents of waste and abuse. 

Recovery and Escalation Procedures: Confirmed cases of provider fraud lead to recovery actions, potential suspension, and, in severe cases, removal from the network; member fraud results in blocking and notification to account management and underwriting teams. 

Regulatory Constraints on Payment Prevention: While technical mechanisms exist to hold or prevent payments, regulatory requirements mandate timely payments, so the team typically withholds a portion for reconciliation and escalates only in proven cases of fraud. 

Validation, Testing, and Limitations of Rule-Based Systems: Omar, Dr.Amr, and Arvind discussed the processes for validating SAS rules, the team's confidence in rule coverage, and the limitations of current rule-based approaches, including the absence of back-testing on historical data and the need for more advanced analytics. 

Rule Validation and Testing: Rules are thoroughly tested on live and test data, with multiple permutations and manual checks before deployment, and require governance team approval to ensure accuracy and coverage. 

Confidence and Limitations: The team reports high confidence in SAS's ability to capture claims matching rule criteria, though acknowledges occasional bugs or missed cases, and notes that scoring mechanisms exist but are not actively used in decision-making. 

No Back-Testing on Historical Data: Currently, new rules are not retroactively applied to historical claims, though manual reviews of past claims are conducted for recovery purposes; participants discussed the potential value of such back-testing for provider negotiations and internal analysis. 

Need for Advanced Analytics: There is consensus that rule-based systems have inherent limitations and that integrating advanced analytics or AI could help identify patterns and relationships not currently captured by manual or rule-based methods.

Process Mapping and Gap Analysis: The team will document the current and desired future processes, identify gaps, and engage with the technology team to discuss potential pilot projects and phased implementation strategies. 

Pilot and Incremental Implementation: Plans include deploying a proof-of-concept or soft pilot for select scenarios, evaluating outcomes, and scaling up in manageable phases to ensure successful adoption and measurable improvements.
